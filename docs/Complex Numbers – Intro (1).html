1. Introduction to Complex Numbers

1.1 Definition

A complex number is defined as z=a+jb,a,b∈R,j2=−1. Here:

  • a is called the real part.
  • b is called the imaginary part.
  • We write often ℜ{z}=a, ℑ{z}=b.

1.2 Real & Imaginary Parts

For any z=a+jb:

  • Real part: ℜ{z}=a.
  • Imaginary part: ℑ{z}=b.
Equivalently, we write z=a+jb=ℜ{z}+jℑ{z}.

1.3 Argand Diagram

We can identify each complex number z=a+jb with the point (a,b) in the plane. That 2D‐plane is called the Argand diagram (or complex plane).

Key geometric ideas:

  • Plot z=a+jb at coordinate (a,b).
  • The horizontal axis is the real axis; the vertical axis is the imaginary axis.
  • Geometric operations (e.g.\ addition) become vector‐based. We will see more in Section 3.

2. Rectangular Form

When working in rectangular form, we keep complex numbers as a+jb. Addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division are easiest in this form.

2.1 Addition / Subtraction

If z1=a+jbandz2=c+jd, then z1+z2=(a+jb)+(c+jd)=(a+c)+j(b+d),z1−z2=(a+jb)−(c+jd)=(a−c)+j(b−d).

Example: (4+3j)−(2−6j)=(4−2)+j(3−(−6))=2+j9.

2.2 Multiplication

Again let z1=a+jb and z2=c+jd. Then z1×z2=(a+jb)(c+jd)=(ac−bd)+j(ad+bc).

Example: (1+2j)(4+6j)=(1⋅4−2⋅6)+j(1⋅6+2⋅4)=(4−12)+j(6+8)=−8+j14.

Alternatively, if you already know the polar/phasor form of each factor, you can multiply magnitudes and add angles (see Section 3). But purely in rectangular form, just use the “ac−bd” (real part) and “ad+bc” (imaginary part) rule shown above.

2.3 Division

To compute z1z2 with z1=a+jb, z2=c+jd, multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate of z2: a+jbc+jd=(a+jb)(c−jd)(c+jd)(c−jd)=(ac+bd)+j(bc−ad)c2+d2.

Example: 2+4j6−7j=(2+4j)(6+7j)(6−7j)(6+7j)=12+28+j(24−14)36+49=40+j1085=817+j217.

3. Magnitude & Argument (Polar Form)

In polar (or phasor) form, we express z=a+jb by its magnitude |z| and angle θ=arg⁡(z). Converting back and forth will be covered below.

3.1 Modulus (Magnitude)

If z=a+jb, then the modulus (or absolute value) is |z|=a2+b2. Geometrically, this is the distance from the origin (0,0) to the point (a,b) in the Argand plane.

3.2 Argument (Angle)

The argument θ=arg⁡(z) is the angle (in radians or degrees) that the vector from the origin to (a,b) makes with the positive real axis. Formally, θ=tan−1(ba), but you must pay attention to the correct quadrant depending on the signs of a and b. For example:

  • If a>0, then θ=tan−1⁡(b/a) lies in (−90∘,+90∘).
  • If a<0 and b≥0, then θ=tan−1⁡(b/a)+180∘.
  • If a<0 and b<0, then θ=tan−1⁡(b/a)−180∘.
  • If a=0 and b>0, then θ=+90∘. If a=0 and b<0, then θ=−90∘.

3.3 Polar Form

Once you know |z| and θ=arg⁡(z), you can write: z=a+jb=|z|(cos⁡θ+jsin⁡θ). We often abbreviate this as the “phasor” or “angle” notation: z=|z|∠θ. Equivalently, by Euler’s formula, z=|z|ejθ,since ejθ=cos⁡θ+jsin⁡θ.

Examples (Rectangular → Polar):

  • 4+4j.
    |4+4j|=42+42=32=42,θ=tan−1(44)=45∘. Therefore, 4+4j=42∠45∘.
  • 3+0j.
    |3|=3,θ=0∘⟹3=3∠0∘.
  • −2j.
    |−2j|=2,arg⁡(−2j)=−90∘⟹−2j=2∠(−90∘).
  • −12+3j.
    |−12+3j|=(−12)2+32=144+9=153, θ=tan−1(3−12).Since a<0,b>0,θ=180∘−14.036∘≈165.964∘. Hence, −12+3j=153∠165.96∘.

4. Complex Conjugate

For a complex number z=a+jb, its complex conjugate (denoted z∗ or z―) is z∗=a−jb.

In polar form, if z=rejθ, then z∗=re−jθ.

Key properties of the conjugate (for any two complex numbers z1 and z2):

  • (z1+z2)∗=z1∗+z2∗.
  • (z1z2)∗=z1∗z2∗.
  • (z1z2)∗=z1∗z2∗ (assuming z2≠0).
  • z⋅z∗=a2+b2=|z|2.
✎ Example

Let z=−1+2j. Then:

z=−1+2j⟹z∗=−1−2j, |z|2=(−1)2+(2)2=1+4=5,zz∗=(−1+2j)(−1−2j)=5.
